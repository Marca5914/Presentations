Probiotics in Preterm Infants: Current Evidence and Clinical Implications

A Review for Medical Professionals

Introduction: The Vulnerable Preterm Gut

Preterm infants, particularly those with very low birth weight (VLBW) (<1500g) or extremely low birth weight (ELBW) (<1000g), face a high risk of severe gastrointestinal complications and mortality.

Key Contributing Factors:

  • Immature immune and gastrointestinal systems, making them susceptible to complications.
  • Delayed postnatal acquisition of diverse bowel flora, leading to intestinal dysbiosis.

These vulnerabilities increase the incidence of devastating conditions such as:

  • Necrotising Enterocolitis (NEC): A severe intestinal inflammation. It is a major cause of mortality and morbidity, with mortality rates ranging from 15% to 30%.
  • Late-Onset Sepsis (LOS): A significant cause of mortality and morbidity in preterm infants.

Probiotics, defined as live microorganisms that confer a health benefit when administered in adequate amounts, have emerged as a potential strategy to modulate the intestinal microbiome and improve outcomes in this vulnerable population. The underlying hypothesis is that by fostering a gut flora akin to that of healthy term infants, intestinal barrier function can be enhanced, thereby reducing the incidence of NEC and LOS.

The Journey of Probiotics in Preterm Care

1960s - 1999

Concept of "probiotics" emerges. Early observational reports (1999) and RCTs (1997) show initial promise in reducing NEC.

Early 2000s

First single-site RCTs report significant reductions in NEC, LOS, and death with probiotic mixtures, building early momentum.

2010s

Multiple meta-analyses suggest benefit, but the large, rigorously conducted PiPS trial (2016) finds no evidence of benefit, creating controversy.

2019 - 2025

Network meta-analyses provide nuance, suggesting multi-strain combinations are most effective. Focus shifts to strain-specific benefits (*B. infantis*, *L. rhamnosus GG*).

Current Status

Routine use remains controversial. Despite strong evidence for NEC reduction, concerns about safety, quality control, and optimal protocols persist.

Impact on All-Cause Mortality

Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses consistently suggest that probiotic supplementation reduces all-cause mortality in preterm or low birth weight (LBW) infants.

Specific Findings:

  • A large Cochrane meta-analysis (54 trials, 10,484 infants) concluded that probiotics probably reduce all-cause mortality slightly before hospital discharge (Risk Ratio [RR] 0.77, 95% Confidence Interval [CI] 0.66 to 0.90). The NNTB was 50 (95% CI 50 to 100).
  • A meta-analysis of 23 RCTs (N = 4686) noted a significant reduction in death with Lactobacillus (RR 0.48, 95% CI 0.36–0.64; P < 0.00001).
  • Routine probiotic supplementation (RPS) in non-RCTs was associated with a significantly reduced all-cause mortality (Odds Ratio [OR]: 0.77; 95% CI: 0.68, 0.88).
  • Supplementation with Bifidobacterium breve BBG-001 demonstrated a reduction in overall mortality and morbidity (OR = 0.74; 95% CI [0.62, 0.89]).
  • A systematic review of RCTs in India reported a reduced risk of mortality (RR: 0.62 (95% CI: 0.41, 0.95)).

Important Caveat:

Evidence for extremely low birth weight (ELBW) neonates regarding mortality often lacks statistical significance, which may be attributed to a smaller number of studies focused on this highly vulnerable subgroup.

Impact on Necrotising Enterocolitis (NEC)

Probiotics are widely associated with a significant reduction in the incidence of NEC, particularly severe NEC (Bell stage II or higher).

Key Evidence:

  • A recent Cochrane review (57 trials, 10,918 infants) indicated that probiotics may reduce the risk of NEC (RR 0.54, 95% CI 0.46 to 0.65). The NNTB for NEC was 33 (95% CI 25 to 50).
  • Lactobacillus alone (RR 0.34, 95% CI 0.25–0.46) and combinations of Lactobacillus spp. and Bifidobacterium spp. (OR, 0.35; 95% CI, 0.20–0.59) were particularly effective.
  • Routine probiotic supplementation (RPS) in non-RCTs showed a significant reduction in NEC ≥ Stage II (OR: 0.60; 95% CI: 0.50, 0.73).
  • Bifidobacterium breve BBG-001 specifically reduced NEC stage >2 (OR = 0.66; 95% CI [0.47, 0.94]).

Proposed Mechanisms for NEC Reduction:

  • Improving immunity and reducing inflammation.
  • Enhancing tissue repair and gut barrier function.
  • Correcting intestinal dysbiosis.

Impact on Late-Onset Sepsis (LOS)

The evidence regarding the effect of probiotics on late-onset sepsis (LOS) in preterm infants is less consistent than for NEC.

Conflicting Findings:

  • Some meta-analyses, including a recent Cochrane review, indicate probiotics probably have little or no effect on the risk of LOS (RR 0.89, 95% CI 0.82 to 0.97).
  • An older Cochrane review (2008) and a network meta-analysis also found no significant difference in sepsis incidence.

Evidence Suggesting Reduction:

  • A large meta-analysis (37 RCTs, N = 9416) showed probiotics significantly decreased the risk of LOS (RR, 0.86; 95% CI, 0.78–0.94).
  • RPS in non-RCTs was associated with a significantly reduced LOS (OR: 0.85; 95% CI: 0.74, 0.97).
  • Bifidobacterium breve BBG-001 has been shown to significantly reduce sepsis (OR = 0.71).
  • Meta-analyses in LMICs also reported a reduced risk of LOS (RR 0.80).

For ELBW neonates, specific studies indicate no significant difference in LOS incidence with probiotic use.

Mechanisms of Action

Probiotics are believed to exert their beneficial effects in preterm infants through several key mechanisms:

Gut Microbiota Modulation

Increased abundance of supplemented strains (Bifidobacterium, Lactobacillus) and decrease in potential pathogens (Clostridium, Klebsiella).

Enhancing Intestinal Barrier Function

Strengthens the intestinal mucosal barrier and improves its integrity, preventing bacterial translocation.

Modulating Immune Responses

Regulates immune pathways (TLR4, NF-kB) and inflammatory cytokines for a balanced immune response.

Direct Inhibition of Pathogens

Competes for nutrients and adhesion sites (niche exclusion) and produces antimicrobial metabolites.

Improving Feeding Tolerance

Can improve feeding tolerance and reduce the time to reach full enteral feeds (TFF), improving nutritional outcomes.

Strain Specificity and Combinations

A critical aspect of probiotic efficacy is its strain-specific nature, meaning that benefits can vary significantly between different species and even strains within the same species.

Multi-Strain & Combinations

  • A combination of Lactobacillus + Bifidobacterium species consistently showed moderate- or high-quality evidence of reduced all-cause mortality (OR, 0.56) and severe NEC (OR, 0.35).
  • Multi-strain RPS was reported as more effective than single-strain supplementation in one non-RCT meta-analysis.

Specific Strains with Noted Efficacy

  • Lactobacillus reuteri
  • Lactobacillus rhamnosus (LGG)
  • Bifidobacterium animalis subspecies lactis
  • Bifidobacterium breve BBG-001
  • Probiotics including Bifidobacterium infantis may offer greater benefits.

Safety Concerns

While many studies report no significant adverse effects, concerns about safety, particularly probiotic sepsis, persist and hinder widespread adoption.

Probiotic Sepsis

A recent systematic review identified 16 reports involving 32 preterm infants. The most commonly isolated organisms were Bifidobacterium (n=19), Lactobacillus (n=10), and Saccharomyces (n=3). Most cases recovered, with two reported neonatal deaths.

Identified Risk Factors:

  • Compromised gut barrier: Immature/damaged gut allows for potential bacterial translocation.
  • Product contamination: A documented risk during manufacturing or administration.
  • Vulnerable populations: Critically ill or ELBW infants are at higher risk.

Clinical Considerations:

Caution is advised against initiating or continuing probiotics during suspected/proven LOS and NEC. The lack of FDA-regulated products and insufficient safety data for ELBW infants contribute to a cautious stance.

Current Recommendations and Controversies

Despite accumulating evidence, the routine use of probiotics in preterm infants remains controversial and highly variable across neonatal units worldwide.

Reasons for Controversy:

  • Heterogeneity of trials: Varies in participants, strains, doses, and outcomes.
  • Lack of clarity on optimal protocols: No consensus on strain, dose, or timing.
  • Concerns about product quality and contamination.
  • Need for further robust research, especially for ELBW infants.

Varying Professional Stances:

  • RECOMMEND/CONDITIONAL: American Pediatric Surgical Association, ESPGHAN, American Gastroenterological Association (AGA).
  • CAUTION AGAINST ROUTINE USE: American Academy of Pediatrics (2021), citing lack of pharmaceutical-grade products and safety data for ELBW infants.

Conclusion

  • Probiotic supplementation demonstrates strong evidence for reducing NEC and likely reduces all-cause mortality in preterm infants.
  • The impact on LOS remains less consistent, with conflicting study results.
  • The strain-specific nature of effects and potential benefits of multi-strain combinations are increasingly recognised.
  • While generally safe, the rare but serious risk of probiotic sepsis necessitates careful consideration and rigorous safety protocols.
  • Further high-quality, large-scale RCTs focusing on specific strains, optimal dosages, and outcomes in ELBW infants are essential to inform routine clinical practice worldwide.
